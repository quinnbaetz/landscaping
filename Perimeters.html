Overview
Project · Lafayette landscaping

Perimeters

Shaded perimeter beds — pollinator-friendly, pest-deterrent, aromatic, culinary, medicinal

Goals

  • Small plants. Low-growing perennials and herbs — under ~3 ft. These beds are visual edges, not screens.
  • Shade-tolerant. Partial shade is the default condition (perimeter beds catch building shadows for big chunks of the day).
  • Pollinators. Every bed should have something blooming in spring, summer, and fall.
  • Pest deterrent — especially mosquitos. Lots of aromatic plants whose oils repel mosquitos, gnats, and aphids.
  • Smells great. Brushing past releases scent.
  • Culinary & medicinal. Most of the palette is harvestable — for the kitchen or the medicine cabinet.

Layout

  • Plant in drifts of 3–5 of the same species, not singletons. Single specimens read as weeds; drifts read as design.
  • Tall-ish things (bee balm, catmint, sage) at the back of the bed; mid-height (lemon balm, calendula, chamomile, garlic chives, yarrow) in the middle; groundcovers (sweet woodruff, wild ginger, sweet violet, lungwort) at the front.
  • Confined plants: mints, lemon balm, bee balm — sink bottomless containers (5-gal nursery pots with the bottom cut out) into the bed to keep the runners in.
  • Annual reseeders (calendula, chamomile, sweet violet) will fill in gaps naturally year after year.
  • Mulch lightly (1–2 in compost or fine chips) — heavy mulch smothers the low groundcovers.
